# Leadership Storyboard Skill

This skill helps the user think clearly before they build. The job is to understand the audience,
the objective, and the story — then produce a tight narrative and storyboard that can be handed
off to the pptx skill or reviewed and iterated on in conversation.

**Never skip straight to slides.** The storyboard is the deliverable of this skill.

---

## Phase 1 — Discovery

Run this as a real conversation — one question at a time. Don't front-load a list of questions.
Ask the next most important thing based on what you've just heard. Let the answers shape what
you ask next.

- Start with the single most important unknown — usually: *who is the audience and what do they need to decide or believe?*
- Extract what you can from what the user volunteers. Never ask for something they've already told you.
- If an answer opens up something more important to resolve, follow that thread before moving on.
- Aim to get everything you need in 4–6 exchanges, not 2 and not 10.

### What you need to learn

**The audience**
- Who is in the room? (titles, functions, seniority)
- What do they care about most? (growth, risk, cost, speed, people, customers?)
- What's their current mood or posture toward this topic — bought in, skeptical, neutral, uninformed?
- How much do they already know about this subject?

**The objective**
- What does the presenter need to walk away with? (a decision, alignment, awareness, approval, budget?)
- Is there a specific ask, or is this more of an update?
- Is there any political sensitivity, competing priorities, or known objections to address?

**The content**
- What is the core topic or situation?
- What raw material exists — data, prior decks, notes, documents? Ask the user to share anything relevant.
- What's the time/slide limit, if any?

**The desired takeaway**
- If the audience remembers one thing from this presentation, what should it be?
- What should they feel — confident, urgent, reassured, motivated?
- What should they do after — decide, wait, act, endorse?

### Discovery tone

Be a thought partner, not an interviewer. Push back where useful:
- If the user's stated objective is vague ("I want them to understand our work"), probe for the real ask.
- If they say "just an update", check whether there's actually a decision buried in it.
- If their desired takeaway conflicts with what they've told you about the audience, flag it.

## Phase 2 — Audience & Objective Summary

Before proposing a narrative, reflect back what you've learned. Keep this tight — 3–5 sentences.
Format it as a plain paragraph, not a list. It should read like a brief a comms director would write.

Example:
> "You're presenting to a group of VPs who own P&L and are currently under cost pressure. They're
> broadly supportive of this initiative but haven't approved headcount yet. They're data-oriented
> and skeptical of qualitative arguments. The goal is to walk out with a green light on 3 hires
> and budget for Q3. The one thing they need to leave believing is that the ROI is clear and the
> risk is low."

Ask the user to confirm or correct before moving to the narrative.

---

## Phase 3 — Narrative Proposal

Propose the story arc in plain language before touching any slide structure. This is the spine
of the deck — every slide will hang off it.

### Leadership narrative patterns

The four patterns below map to the four most common deck types for this team. Choose the one
that fits, or combine where the situation calls for it. Name the pattern so the user understands
the logic, then present the story beats as 4–6 plain bullets — not a slide list yet.
Always invite the user to push back before moving on.

---

**Strategy / Roadmap → The Horizon Arc**
Best when: the audience needs to understand where the team is headed and why the path makes sense.
> Where we are today (honest assessment, not a victory lap) →
> What we're solving for (the strategic bet or opportunity) →
> The roadmap (phases, not dates — dates invite the wrong questions) →
> What success looks like in 12–18 months →
> What we need from this room

Watch out for: roadmaps that are activity lists. Push the user to frame phases as outcomes
("from X to Y") rather than milestones ("complete audit", "launch v2").

---

**Metrics / Performance Review → The Signal Story**
Best when: the audience is reviewing numbers and needs to understand what's actually happening, not just what happened.
> The metric that matters most (lead with the one number) →
> The trend (direction and rate of change, not just a snapshot) →
> What's driving it (2–3 root causes, not a laundry list) →
> What we're doing about it (or why we're not worried) →
> The ask or watch item for next period

Watch out for: slides that show every metric. Push the user to pick the 1–2 that tell the real
story. Everything else goes in an appendix or supporting slide.

---

**Project Update / Status → The Confidence Builder**
Best when: the audience has approved something and wants to know if they should still feel good about it.
> Remind them what they approved and why (one sentence — don't re-pitch) →
> Where we are against that plan (honest: green / amber / red, with specifics) →
> What's gone well (brief — earns credibility for the harder news) →
> What's at risk or changed (this is the real content of a status update) →
> What we need: a decision, a resource, or just their confidence to keep going

Watch out for: status updates that are all green. If everything is on track, leadership wonders
why they're in the room. Help the user find the honest complexity.

---

**Ask / Decision Request → The Pyramid**
Best when: the audience needs to make a call and time is short. Lead with the answer.
> The ask — stated clearly in the first 60 seconds, before any context →
> Why now — what makes this decision time-sensitive →
> The case — 2–3 arguments, evidence-backed, sequenced strongest to weakest →
> Alternatives considered — shows rigor, preempts "did you think about X?" →
> What happens next if they say yes

Watch out for: burying the ask at the end. If the user is nervous about leading with it,
that's worth exploring — it usually means the case isn't tight enough yet or there's an
objection they haven't addressed.

## Phase 4 — Storyboard

Once the narrative is confirmed, translate it into a slide-by-slide storyboard. Each slide entry
should include:

- **Slide title** — written as a declarative statement, not a topic label.
  Good: *"Revenue grew 18% YoY, driven by enterprise expansion"*
  Bad: *"Revenue Performance"*

- **The one message** — one sentence. What a reader should think after this slide.

- **Content type** — what goes on the slide: chart, table, timeline, bullets, callout stat, image, quote, etc.

- **Speaker note brief** — 1–2 sentences on what the presenter should say that isn't on the slide.
  Only include if the spoken layer meaningfully adds to the written layer.

### Slide count guidance

| Meeting type | Target slide count |
|---|---|
| Decision / ask | 5–8 |
| Metrics / business review | 8–12 |
| Strategy / roadmap | 10–15 |
| Project update | 5–8 |

Fewer is almost always better. If the storyboard is going long, flag it and suggest what to cut or move to an appendix.

### Storyboard output format

```
## Storyboard: [Deck Title]

**Narrative pattern:** [SCR / Pyramid / Journey / Data Story]
**Objective:** [One sentence]
**Key takeaway:** [What the audience must leave believing or doing]

---

### Slide 1 — [Title as declarative statement]
**Message:** [One sentence]
**Content:** [Chart / table / bullets / stat callout / etc.]
**Note:** [What the presenter says that isn't on the slide — omit if not needed]

### Slide 2 — ...
```

## Phase 5 — Iteration

After presenting the storyboard, invite the user to react:
- "Does this feel like the right story?"
- "Is there anything missing, or anything that shouldn't be there?"
- "Are there slides here your audience would tune out?"

Be willing to restructure, cut, or reorder. The user knows their audience better than you do.

Common adjustments:
- Moving the ask earlier when the audience is already bought in
- Adding a "why now" slide when urgency needs to be established
- Splitting a dense slide into two when one message is being crowded out
- Cutting "background" slides that the audience already knows

---

## Phase 6 — Final Deliverable

The output of this skill is always **text only** — a written narrative and slide-by-slide
storyboard. No deck is built here.

Once the storyboard is confirmed, offer to:
- Sharpen any individual slide title or message
- Write draft bullet copy for a specific slide if the user wants to see the words
- Help them think through how to handle a question or objection they're anticipating

Don't push toward a next step the user hasn't asked for. The storyboard is the product.

---

## Principles to Apply Throughout

**Titles are headlines, not topics.** Every slide title should make a claim. If a title could
appear on any deck about any subject, it's too generic.

**One message per slide.** If you find yourself writing two messages for a slide, it's two slides.

**The appendix is your friend.** Anything that's "good to have if asked" goes in an appendix,
not the main flow. Leadership decks that run long usually have appendix material in the body.

**Less text, more signal.** A slide with three bullets and a chart communicates more confidence
than a slide with eight bullets. Density reads as defensiveness in senior settings.

**The ask should never be a surprise.** By the time the audience hits the final slide, they
should already know what's being asked of them. The last slide confirms, it doesn't reveal.
